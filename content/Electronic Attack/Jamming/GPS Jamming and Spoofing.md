# Overview
GPS jamming and spoofing are two methods used to interfere with Global Positioning System (GPS) signals. Although they are often mentioned together, they accomplish different objectives. GPS jamming blocks satellite signals from reaching a receiver, causing the device to lose its location entirely. GPS spoofing is more advanced because it sends false GPS signals that trick the receiver into believing it is somewhere else. Both methods are commonly used during military operations to disrupt navigation, precision-guided weapons, unmanned aircraft systems (UAS), and communication networks that depend on accurate positioning (Trimble, 2025).

# How it Works
GPS satellites constantly transmit weak radio signals to receivers on Earth. Because these signals travel such long distances, they are relatively easy to interfere with. A jammer simply broadcasts stronger radio frequencies on the same channel, overpowering the legitimate GPS signal until the receiver can no longer calculate its location. Spoofing takes this one step further by transmitting counterfeit GPS signals that appear legitimate, causing the receiver to calculate an incorrect position without realizing it has been manipulated (Trimble, 2025).

# Why GPS jamming is important
Modern military forces rely heavily on GPS for navigation, targeting, logistics, and synchronization between friendly units. By denying or manipulating GPS signals, an adversary can reduce the effectiveness of aircraft, drones, missiles, and ground vehicles while creating confusion throughout the battlefield. Because many modern weapon systems depend on satellite navigation, GPS jamming and spoofing have become some of the most valuable tools available within Electronic Attack operations. As electronic warfare continues to evolve, protecting against these attacks has become just as important as conducting them (Trimble, 2025; Lichtman et al., 2016).
